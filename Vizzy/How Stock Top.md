In the book "The Best", the late Kevin Marder interviewed Dr. Chris Kacher and Gil Morales - of the "pocket pivot" fame" - regarding their monstrous tens of thousands of percent returns. In the interview, Morales outlined the three main ways that stocks top. His insights offer critical clues to how traders can understand these topping warning signs and act appropriately to protect their capital and gains.

## 1. Climax Top – The Blow-Off Ending

What it is: A climax top, or blow-off top, is a vertical surge in price that often ends a major advance. The move is typically fast, parabolic, and unsustainable, with massive volume. It often attracts late buyers driven by FOMO, right before the stock reverses hard.

Characteristics:

- A steep upside acceleration after an already-strong uptrend.
    
- Volume expands significantly, signaling institutional selling into strength.
    
- Often gaps up or makes multiple wide-range bars in a short time.
    
- Can coincide with news or an earnings event.
    
- Price may reverse intraday or over 1–3 days with reversal candles (e.g., bearish engulfing, outside bar).
    

What to watch for:

- A climax gap up followed by a reversal on volume is a red flag.
    
- Intraday reversals after a vertical move may offer short entries.
    
- Failure to hold 10-day or 20-day moving average confirms topping action.
    

## 2. Wedging Top – The Slow Death

What it is: A wedging top occurs when a stock grinds higher on weakening volume and momentum. It’s a deceptive form of topping because the stock keeps making new highs, but with less “juice.” Institutions aren’t buying anymore—retail often is.

Characteristics:

- Price rises in a narrowing, rising wedge.
    
- Volume dries up even as new highs are made.
    
- Breakouts lack follow-through.
    
- Momentum indicators (e.g., RSI, MACD) diverge negatively.
    
- Often precedes a sudden breakdown below the 20- or 50-day moving average.
    

What to watch for:

- Failure to make meaningful progress after breakout attempts.
    
- RS line stalling or turning down before price does.
    
- A decisive breach of key MAs (20d/50d) confirms distribution is underway.
    

Trading insight: Wedging can trap bulls. It’s not obvious until it fails—but early warnings include choppy action, low-volume new highs, and distribution days in the broader market.

## 3. Failed Base Breakout – The Bull Trap

What it is: A failed breakout occurs when a stock clears a base or resistance level (such as a cup-with-handle or flat base), but quickly reverses and breaks down. This is a hallmark of exhausted buying power or institutional distribution.

Characteristics:

- Stock breaks out on volume, then fails to follow through.
    
- Within days (sometimes same day), reverses and falls below breakout point.
    
- Often closes back in the base or under pivot—invalidating the pattern.
    
- Can lead to sharp downside moves, especially if the breakout was widely watched.
    

What to watch for:

- If price fails to hold the pivot point or previous highs after a breakout, beware.
    
- Tight stops are critical here. Breakouts that quickly reverse are often “bull traps.”
    
- Heavier volume on the breakdown suggests institutional selling.
    

Tactical takeaway:

You’ll often see this when the general market is under distribution, or when sentiment gets too frothy. Avoid chasing extended breakouts and wait for confirmation (e.g., hold above pivot, tight price action, supportive market).


## Integrating the Framework

Gil and Chris, rooted in the O’Neil/Minervini tradition but with their own twist, emphasize price/volume behavior over narrative. These topping patterns are less about news and more about how a stock behaves at key inflection points.

When you pair this knowledge with tools like:

- Moving averages (10d, 20d, 50d),
    
- Relative strength vs. SPY or QQQ,
    
- Volume signatures and
    
- Intraday price action,
    

— you get early warnings to exit, reduce, or even short.
